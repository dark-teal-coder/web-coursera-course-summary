Video: Introduction: Investing Basics

  • WHY do you want to invest in the first place?
  • Selecting an investment strategy largely depends on your financial goals.
  • Examples of financial goals:
    • building an emergency fund
    • investing for retirement
    • saving for a home down payment
    • starting a business or family

Video: Stock Basics

  • A stock is a portion of ownership of a company.
  • Stocks are also known as shares of a company, or equity investments, and are primarily bought and sold on publicly traded stock exchanges.
  • There are 2 common ways that you can earn money through stock ownership.
    1. Value/Capital appreciation
      • They're worth more when the business overall value increases and may also command higher market prices due to demand.
      • That means you can earn money by selling your stock at a profit.
    2. Dividend payments
      • Stockholders might earn dividends of a company's profit, which may be paid in cash or in additional stock.
  • Stock market: The stock market is where investors connect to buy and sell investments.
  • A stock is a share of ownership of a company which you can purchase electronically.

Video: Understanding Stocks

  • Each share of stock represents a small but equal share in the company.
  • The stocks you can buy are in public companies that have registered their stock with the US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).
  • A diversified portfolio will help to mitigate the risk of being too heavily invested in any one category.
  • Stocks are sometimes referred to as equities.
  • Owners of stocks are often referred to as stockholders or shareholders.
  • Stocks come in 2 varieties
    1. Common stocks: Along with proportional ownership of the company and the growth potential, common stocks also offer voting rights to the stockholder (e.g., management elections or structural business changes).
    2. Preferred stocks don't come with voting rights, but they're getting preferred status in the earnings that are paid out to preferred stockholders first.
  • Market capitalization: the market value of a company's publicly traded stock.
    • Larger companies/cap stocks: older, more established, and less risky for investors
    • Smaller companies/cap stocks: often newer, less established, riskier, and more growth potential
  • Stocks classified in different style categories:
    • Value stocks: stocks considered to be under value
    • Growth stocks: companies expected to continue growing at a faster rate than their competitors
    • Dividend investing: a style utilized by investors targeting stocks' ability to produce income or yield high dividends and provide cash flow (e.g., utility and real estate companies)

Video: Analyzing Stocks

  • The value of stock is made up of several factors:
    • the company's ability to continue making a profit
    • its customer base
    • its financial structure
    • the economy
    • political and cultural trends
    • how the company fits within a broader industry
  • Ratios can also offer insight into a company's financial health, allowing for comparisons to other companies in the same industry or against the overall market.
    • Price to earning ratio (P ratio):
      • It divides the market price of a company's stock by the company's earning per share.
      • It can reveal how many years it'll take for a company to generate enough value to buy back its stock.
    • Price to sales ratio:
      • It divides the market capitalization of the company by its revenue.
      • It should be as close to one as possible. If it's less than one, it's considered excellent.
    • Earnings per share (EPS)
      • It is calculated by taking the net income, subtracting any preferred stock dividends, and dividing the result by the total number of outstanding common stock shares.
      • It lets you know how much earnings each shareholder would receive if the company was liquidated immediately.
      • Rising EPS means the company's potential to more money to distribute to shareholders or roll back into the business.
    • Return on equity (ROE)
      • It is determined by dividing the company's net income by the shareholder's equity, then multiplying it by 100.
      • It measures the growth and profit of a company.
      • It tells you the value you receive as a shareholder should the company liquidate.

Video: Investing in Stocks

  • Stocks might lose value under a few circumstances.
    • The issuing company falters or goes under.
    • If the market as a whole experience losses due to wider-reaching occurrences like economic recessions, war, or political changes.
    • Scary news and headlines
  • Stocks are volatile and their value can fluctuate in wide swings, but the expectation is that they will still trend upwards over time.
  • In order to reduce risk, keep portfolio diversification with stocks across a range of sectors and risks.

01.02 Understanding Bonds

Video: Bond Basics

  • Bonds are loans you make to either a company or a government entity for a fixed period of time.
  • They pay you fixed interests until they pay you the money back in the future.
  • Three common types of bonds:
    • Treasury bonds, which are issued by the US Government (less risky)
    • Corporate bonds, which are issued by corporations (higher returns)
    • Municipal bonds, which are issued by a state or local government agency, e.g., airports, school districts, and sewer and water authorities
  • Two key benefits of purchasing bonds:
    • Reliable source of income
    • Lower risks used to reduce volatility of a portfolio
  • You can also access bonds through bond mutual funds or even bond ETFs.

Video: Understanding Bonds

  • Corporate bonds are rated for risk by agencies such as Standard and Poor and Moody's.
  • The precise scale varies with the rating agency.
    • Bonds rated AAA to BBB by S&P are considered investment grade.
  • As a rule, investors demand higher interest on lower quality bonds. High-yield bonds yield more because the risk of default is higher.
  • Yield is the income generated on a given investment over a set period of time.
    • Expressed as a percentage in annual terms
    • The interest paid on that bond typically stated on the bond itself
  • Yield to maturity calculates the average return for the bond when it matures based on the purchase price.
  • If the interest rates on the bond (also the coupon rate) are higher than that on bonds issued today, the bond value will be at a premium or higher than par value.
  • If the rate on the bond is lower than that on newly issued bonds, the bond will trade at a discount or lower than the price of the par value.
  • The primary way to make money on bonds is by receiving interest while owning the bond.
  • Another way that you can make money on a bond is by buying it at a discount and holding it to maturity.

Video: Analyzing Bonds

  • An issuer is the entity that borrowed the money.
  • Maturity is how long the bondholders have to wait for their money.
    • The longer, the higher the interest rate, but also the greater the risk
  • Quality is the financial strength of the issuer.
  • Prices and interest rates tend to go in different directions.
  • If you are trying to sell your bond before it matures:
    • Higher interest rates result in lower bond prices.
    • Lower interest rates result in higher bond prices.
  • Bonds:
    • Lower risk
    • Reduced price volatility
    • More predictable yield
    • Referred to as fixed income investments
  • The underlying price of the bond can change so it is possible to experience negative returns.

Video: Investing in Bonds

  • Two reasons to buy bonds:
    1. Income
      • Reliable income sources: bonds pay interest at a fixed rate
      • Welcome in retirement
    2. Safety
      • High-quality investment grade bonds are typically safer because the borrower is less likely to default on their promise to repay your investment.
  • It usually makes more sense to invest in bond mutual funds or exchange-traded funds, ETFs
  • If the issuing corporation goes bankrupt, bondholders can still expect to be repaid before preferred stockholders.
  • Some bonds have a tax advantage.
  • Investors need to keep up with inflation.
  • Bonds:
    • A way to mitigate risk and volatility of a stock portfolio
    • More suitable for investors with a lower risk appetite or shorter time horizon

Reading: Understanding The Yield to Maturity Formula

  • Article: Guide to Yield to Maturity (YTM)
  • Yield to Maturity (YTM) represents the total return expected from holding a bond until it matures, factoring in interest payments and principal repayment.
  • Calculating YTM involves the bond’s coupon rate, face value, current market price, and time to maturity, making it a complex formula.
    • Coupon rate (or bond's interest rate): the regular payment issuers pay bondholders for the right to borrow their money
      • The higher the coupon rate, the higher the yield.
    • Face value (or par value): the amount paid to a bondholder at its maturity date
    • Market price: how much an investor would have to pay for a bond on the open market currently
      • The price buyers pay on the secondary market may be higher or lower than a bond’s face value. The higher the price of the bond, the lower the yield.
    • Maturity date: the date when the issuer repays the principal is known as the maturity date
  • YTM is useful for comparing bonds with different characteristics, helping investors anticipate returns and understand interest rate risks associated with bond investments.
  • Limitations of YTM include assumptions about reinvestment of interest payments and the neglect of taxes, which can significantly affect actual returns.
  • Investors can utilize YTM as a tool for decision-making but should consider diversifying their portfolios and possibly consulting financial professionals for guidance.
  • Yield to Maturity (YTM) Formula:\[YTM=\frac{C + (FV - PV) \div t}{(FV + PV) \div 2}\]
    • C = Interest or coupon payment
    • FV = Face value of the investment
    • PV = Present value or current price of the investment
    • t = Years it takes the investment to reach the full value or maturity

01.03 Assessing Pooled Investments

Video: What is a Mutual Fund?

  • One efficient way to seek diversification is through the use of mutual funds.
  • Mutual funds: pools filled with different types of securities, such as stocks and bonds
  • Mutual funds can be managed both actively or passively.
    • Passively managed funds attempt to track an index, e.g., the Russell 2000 (an index of 2000 small cap US companies).
    • Actively managed funds attempt to beat the performance of an index.
      • Problem: actively managed funds typically cost you more

Video: All-In-One Mutual Funds

  • Some common investment strategies include asset class funds or sector funds.
    • Asset class funds: groups of similar assets sharing similar risks, e.g., stocks, bonds, cash, or real estate
    • Sector funds: funds representing all or most of the stocks in a particular sector or industry
  • Target date fund: funds designed with dates in mind for when you would sell out of the fund
    • They assume that you'll terminate the fund at some year in the future, usually when you retire, and then shift to a less risky investment as the target year approaches.

Video: What is an Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)?

  • A good alternative to actively managed funds are exchange traded funds (ETFs).
  • Traditional mutual funds are issued by the fund sponsor when you buy them and redeemed when you sell them.
  • They're priced on once a day and after the market closes, at the value of all the underlying securities in the fund, divided by the number of shares, which is their net asset value.
  • Unlike mutual funds, ETFs trade on stock exchanges throughout the day.
  • You buy and sell them to another investor, just like a stock.
  • 2 advantages of ETFs:
    • liquidity: be bought or sold whenever the market is open
    • cost: lower expenses

Reading: Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) vs Mutual Funds: Learning the Difference

  • Article: ETFs vs Mutual Funds: Learning the Difference
  • Differences Between ETFs and Mutual Funds:
    • How to Buy Mutual Funds and ETFs
      • Mutual funds transact once per day, with all investors selling or buying shares at the same closing price.
      • ETFs trade throughout the day on public exchanges, with many shares exchanging hands at various prices as buyers and sellers react to changes in the market.
    • Data on Holdings
      • Mutual funds
        • to report the total value of their portfolio once per day after the stock markets close
        • When investors buy or sell a share of the mutual fund, they transact at net asset value (NAV) at the end of the day.
      • ETFs
        • to report their holdings on a daily basis
        • The price fluctuates throughout the day based on market conditions and the value of the ETF’s underlying holdings.
    • Passive vs. Active
      • ETFs: passive investments
      • Mutual funds: active investments
    • Fee Differences Between ETFs vs Mutual Funds
      • Mutual funds: different types of fees
      • ETFs: lower fees
    • Tax Implications of ETFs vs. Mutual Funds
      • ETFs:
        • capital gains taxes and dividend income taxes
        • more tax efficient
        • fewer transactions to tax
    • Lower Initial Investment
      • Mutual funds: higher initial investment

01.04 Uncovering Advanced Vehicles

Video: What is a Derivative?

  • Derivatives: financial contracts like options, futures and swaps
    1. Options: contracts/agreements that gives an investor the right or the option of buying or selling a security at a set price by a specific date
      • When options contracts are traded publicly, options contract typically represents the right to buy or sell 100 shares.
      • 2 main components of options:
        • strike price: the price at which the shares would be exchanged
        • expiration date: the last date when the options holder can exercise their option, or basically execute on the trade
      • 2 basic types of options:
        • Calls: A call gives an investor the right to buy an asset.
          • An investor who purchases the call is placing a bet that the price of the underlying security will go up. So having the call option gives them the ability to purchase the security at a lower price should that price rise.
        • Puts: A put option gives the investor the right to sell an asset.
          • The investor who purchases a put option may believe the security price will fall and want to lock in the option to sell it at a higher price, should that happen.
      • 3 scenarios for when an investor holds a contract:
        • In-the-money:
          • Profitable option
          • The stock is above the strike price for a call or below the strike price for a put.
          • In either scenario, the contract would result in a profit if exercised today.
        • Out-of-the-money:
          • The stock is trading lower than the strike price for a call and higher than the strike price for a put.
          • Exercising the option would lose money.
        • At-the-money:
          • The strike price of an option and the market price are about the same.
    2. Forwards and futures: contracts for buyers to purchase an asset at a pre determined price on a specific date in the future
      • These are often used for commodities like oil, and used to control costs of business operations.
    3. Swaps: contracts that allow two parties to exchange components of cash, like interest rate and currencies

Reading: 5 Important Options Trading Strategies

  • Article: 10 Options Trading Strategies for All Investors
  • When trading options, investors can either buy existing contracts, or they can “write” or sell contracts for securities they currently hold.
  • 10 Options Trading Strategies:
    1. Long Calls (beginner)
      • e.g., Let’s say an investor believes that stock A will rise in one month. Stock A is currently trading at $10 a share and the investor believes it will rise above $12. The investor could buy an option with a $12 strike price and with an expiration date at least one month from now. If stock A’s price rises to hit $12 within a month, the value or “premium” of the option would likely rise.
    2. Long Puts (beginner)
      • e.g., An investor who buys shares of Tech Stock C currently trading at $20 is nervous about the stock falling, so they buy puts with a strike price of $18 and an expiration two months from now.
        A month later, Tech Stock C stock tumbles to $15, and the investor needs to sell their shares for extra cash. The investor was able to sell the shares at $18 by exercising their puts.
    3. Covered Calls (beginner)
    4. Short Puts (beginner)
    5. Short Calls or Naked Calls (intermediate)
    6. Straddles and Strangles (intermediate)
    7. Cash-Secured Puts (intermediate)
    8. Bull Put Spreads (advanced)
    9. Iron Condors (advanced)
    10. Butterfly Spreads (advanced)
  • Options trading strategies offer a way to potentially profit in almost any market situation — whether prices are going up, down, or sideways.

Video: Equity Compensation - Stock Options

  • 2 most common offers: employee stock options and restricted stock units
  • Stock options give an employee the opportunity to purchase shares of their company's stock at a discount, also called the exercise price.
  • You would want to exercise the option to buy the stock when the exercise price is lower than the market value of that stock.
  • The exercise price is generally predetermined when you receive the grant.

Reading: How Do Employee Stock Options Work?

  • Article: How Do Employee Stock Options Work?
  • Employee stock options (ESO) give an employee the chance to purchase a set number of shares in the company at a set price — often called the exercise price — over a set amount of time.
  • This gives an employee the chance to exercise their ESOs at a point when the exercise price is lower than the market price.
  • Employee Stock Option Basics
    • Exercise price/grant price/strike price: This is the given set price at which employees can purchase the stock options.
    • Market price: This is the current price of the stock on the market (which may be lower or higher than the exercise price).
      • Typically an employee would only choose to exercise and purchase the options if the market price is higher than the grant price.
    • Issue date: This is the date on which you’re given the options.
    • Vesting date: This is the date after which you can exercise your options per the original terms or vesting schedule.
    • Exercise date: This is the date you actually choose to exercise your options.
    • Expiration date: This is the date on which your ability to exercise your options expires.
  • Reason companies offer stock options:
    • If employees are financially invested in the success of the company, then they’re more likely to be emotionally invested in its success as well, and it can increase employee productivity.
  • 2 main kinds of employee stock options, each of which has different tax implications:
    • qualified or incentive stock options (ISOs)
      • Taxes are due at the time you sell the stock and make a profit.
    • non-qualified or non-qualified stock options (NSOs or NQSOs)

02.01 Comparing Different Types of Risk

Video: Understanding Investment Risk

  • Risk: the uncertainty that our realized return will not be the same as our expected return
  • The level of risk increases, so does the potential reward.
  • The more risk you're willing to take, the more you could gain and the more you could lose.
  • Risk appetite: an investor's level of comfort taking risk
    • Tied to you
  • Risk tolerance: how much risk that investor can take on a particular investment based on their goal and time horizon
    • Tied to your individual investments
  • How much risk is appropriate will depend on the combination of your risk appetite and risk tolerance.

Video: Systematic Investment Risk

  • 2 broad categories of risks: systematic risk and unsystematic risk
  • Systematic risk:
    • non-diversifiable risk
    • inescapable or unavoidable
    • inherent in the stock market
    • present regardless of your choice of investments
    • different types of systematic risks:
      • purchasing power risk: loss of purchasing power through inflation
      • reinvestment risk: the risk that proceeds from an investment may have to be reinvested at a lower rate than what you had already been earning
      • interest rate risk: the risk that a change in interest rate will cause the market value of a security that you own to fall
      • market risk: the risk of the overall market
      • exchange rate risk: the risk associated with changes in the value of currencies
      • country risk or political risk: the uncertainty of returns caused by the potential of major changes in the political or economic environment of a country

Video: Unsystematic Investment Risk

  • Unsystematic risk:
    • diversifiable risk
    • 2 types of unsystematic risks:
      • business risk: the nature of a business's operation
      • financial risk: credit risk or default risk - how a business finances its assets and its operations
  • Diversifiable risk: the impact of these risks be reduced through diversification

02.02 Analyzing Investment Risk

Video: Measuring Investment Risk

  • The main measures of investment risk:
    • Beta: the volatility of investment relative to a benchmark
      • The benchmark would often be the whole market or market index.
      • Beta = 1: the investment is as volatile as the benchmark itself
      • Beta < 1: the investment is less volatile than the benchmark
      • Beta > 1: the investment is more volatile than the benchmark
    • Standard deviation: the data dispersion relative to the average or mean value of a set of data
      • Low standard deviation: more predictable investment performance
      • High standard deviation: less predictable or more volatile performance
    • Sharpe ratio: the risk adjusted return
      • To compare the investment return to an investment that is deemed risk free, e.g., treasury bond
      • To assess whether the risk is worth taking for the additional return on a particular investment

Reading: Explaining the Different Types of Asset Classes

  • Article: Explaining the Different Types of Asset Classes
  • Different asset classes:
    • Stocks (equities): a single ownership share in a publicly-traded company (a company that trades on a stock exchange)
      • You can receive dividends from stocks if a company pays out part of its profits, or you might get capital gains when you sell if the price of the stock has risen.
    • Bonds (fixed income): loans you make to a company or government for a predetermined amount of time at a certain amount of interest
      • e.g., treasury bonds, corporate bonds, municipal bonds
    • Money market accounts or cash equivalents: savings account, or certificate of deposit (CD)
      • You get paid interest on the money for lending money to the financial institution.
    • Real estate: buying real estate for the purposes of renting the property to generate income or to earn profits as the value of the property appreciates
    • Commodities: putting money into metals, energy products, livestock, agricultural products, and so forth
      • Commonly through a futures contract
      • Future contract: an agreement to buy or sell a certain commodity at a specific quantity at a predetermined price at a later time
    • Cryptocurrencies: investing in digital currency that is largely unregulated
      • Typically dependant on a direct financial exchange between users, with no involvement from a bank or other third party
    • Real estate investment trusts (REITs): investing primarily in real estate or real estate loans, traded like stocks
